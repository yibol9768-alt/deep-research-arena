\title{Four Empirical Findings from \arena{}:\\
A Deep-Research Agent Benchmark}

\author{Deep Research Arena Team}
\date{April 2026}

\begin{document}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
We report four empirical findings from \arena{}, a sandboxed benchmark for
deep-research agents over a frozen 200k-document corpus (Magento storefront
+ Postmill forum + 49\,GB English Wikipedia). Across 13 agents (mix of
GLM-4.5/4.6/5.1, DeepSeek-chat, Qwen-3.5-plus, GPT-5-chat-latest)
$\times$ 4 cross-site tasks (52 runs), we find (F1) our LLM judge does
\emph{not} reward length --- Spearman $\rho = -0.30$ between judge score
and answer word count --- refuting a common reviewer concern; (F2) URL
hallucination is an architectural artefact concentrated in 4 specific
agents (100\% halluc rate each) rather than a property of any LLM backend;
(F3) Item Response Theory agent-ability rankings match Bradley--Terry Elo
rankings with Spearman $\rho = 1.00$, evidencing internal consistency of
our ranking methodology; (F4) agents sharing identical search-shim results
produce citation distributions that differ by an order of magnitude in
source diversity, depending on whether the agent uses tool-calling or
RAG-compilation architecture.

Together these findings support the argument that sandboxed, multi-pillar,
cross-family-judge evaluation surfaces diagnostic signal unavailable to
single-score live-web benchmarks.
\end{abstract}

%================================================================
\section{Setup}
%================================================================

\arena{} contains three sandbox services on a single host:
\texttt{localhost:7770} (Magento, 540k products),
\texttt{localhost:9999} (Postmill, 25 subreddits),
\texttt{localhost:8090} (Kiwix, English Wikipedia \emph{nopic} 49\,GB).
All agents retrieve through a unified shim exposing a Tavily- and
Firecrawl-compatible API, so any framework that can be pointed at
\texttt{TAVILY\_API\_URL} or \texttt{FIRECRAWL\_BASE\_URL} works
zero-code-change.

\textbf{Agents (13).} \texttt{react-qwen35plus}, \texttt{gpt-researcher},
\texttt{react-glm5}, \texttt{deerflow-glm46-shim}, \texttt{camel-ai},
\texttt{deerflow-glm46-new}, \texttt{deerflow-glm46}, \texttt{camel-ai-ds},
\texttt{smolagents} (GLM), \texttt{odr-ds}, \texttt{smolagents-ds},
\texttt{deerflow-ds}, \texttt{gpt-researcher-ds}. An additional
\texttt{gpt5chat} agent (GPT-5-chat-latest, OpenAI-Agents-SDK-style
tool-calling loop) was integrated for the source-diversity experiment
(F4); it is not included in F1--F3 unless noted.

\textbf{Tasks (4 for F1--F3).}
\texttt{dr\_cross\_v3\_0001} (headphones shopping+reddit),
\texttt{0005} (\$500 home office, shop$\times$4 + reddit$\times$2),
\texttt{0006} (PC gaming, shop$\times$3 + reddit$\times$2),
\texttt{0007} (budget home cook, shop$\times$2 + reddit$\times$2).
Scholarly subset (0088--0107, $n=20$) is introduced for F4.

\textbf{Scoring.} Seven weighted pillars form the composite
(\texttt{markdown\_structure} 0.05, \texttt{citation} 0.20,
\texttt{fact\_kg} 0.15, \texttt{llm\_judge} 0.20, \texttt{checklist} 0.20,
\texttt{evidence\_density} 0.15, \texttt{efficiency} 0.05).
\texttt{llm\_judge} uses DeepResearch-Bench RACE's four dimensions
\citep{dr-bench-2026} under a cross-family judge (GLM-4.5 evaluates only
DeepSeek-backed or Qwen-backed agents, and vice versa) to suppress
self-preference bias \citep{wataoka-2024}.

\section{F1: LLM judge does not reward length}
\label{sec:length}
%================================================================

\paragraph{Premise.}
The verbosity-bias hypothesis \citep{wataoka-2024,arena-hard-2024} states
that LLM-as-judge rewards longer, more confident responses. Several
benchmark papers accept this and either apply length-controlled
normalisation or report only length-matched pairs.

\paragraph{Method.}
For each of our 52 runs we compute the word count of \texttt{answer.md}
(after stripping inline code and URLs) and the Spearman rank correlation
with each pillar score and with the final composite. Correlations are
computed across all runs jointly. Word counts range 191--5384, mean
1540, with clear per-agent style clustering (DeerFlow averages 3162
words; smolagents 991).

\paragraph{Result.}
\begin{table}[h]
  \centering
  \small
  \input{tables/length.tex}
  \caption{Spearman $\rho$ between answer word count and each pillar
    score. All $|\rho| < 0.4$; \texttt{llm\_judge} and \texttt{checklist}
    are \emph{negatively} correlated with length. Regenerated from
    \texttt{data/results/length\_ablation.json}.}
  \label{tab:length}
\end{table}

\paragraph{Why.}
Our RACE-style LLM judge explicitly considers
\textit{instruction-following} and \textit{readability}: a long
meandering report that restates the question multiple times and
over-hedges is penalised on both dimensions. Similarly, our DRACO-style
rubric contains binary items such as ``Does the report identify exactly
$k$ distinct products?'' which a verbose report is more likely to
miss or confuse.

\paragraph{Takeaway.}
Reviewers of deep-research benchmarks almost always ask whether the judge
is length-biased. For \arena{}'s specific setup (GLM-4.5 judge, cross-family
selection, RACE+DRACO dual-rubric), we can answer \textit{no}, with
Table~\ref{tab:length} directly citable. We do not claim this generalises
to all judge configurations --- we recommend every LLM-judge benchmark
report its analogous Spearman table as a sanity check.

%================================================================
\section{F2: URL hallucination is architectural, not model-level}
\label{sec:halluc}
%================================================================

\paragraph{Premise.}
A common narrative treats URL hallucination as ``the LLM makes up links
when uncertain.'' We test whether hallucination is
(a) distributed across all runs proportionally (noise)
or (b) concentrated in specific agents (architectural).

\paragraph{Method.}
Pure heuristic scan of 70 \texttt{answer.md} files
(\texttt{scripts/error\_taxonomy.py}). Each run is placed in exactly one
of four primary classes:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
  \item \texttt{hallucination\_url}: cites $\geq 2$ URLs outside allowed
    sandbox hosts, or contains one of five fake-signal regexes
    (\texttt{(placeholder)}, \texttt{example.com}, live \texttt{wikipedia.org},
    \texttt{*.tavily.*}, \texttt{onestopmarket.com}), or writes a
    $\geq 200$-word report with zero inline citations.
  \item \texttt{format\_error}: answer is JSON-wrapped under 400 words.
  \item \texttt{timeout\_or\_empty}: under 50 words or contains the
    string ``failed to finish''.
  \item \texttt{ok}: none of the above.
\end{itemize}
No LLM calls; pattern matching only. This is deliberately strict: soft
hallucination (right URL, wrong fact in text) is not caught. Hard
hallucination (cited URL does not exist in sandbox) is caught.

\paragraph{Result.}
\begin{table}[h]
  \centering
  \small
  \input{tables/halluc.tex}
  \caption{Per-agent primary failure class distribution. Four agents (bold)
    hallucinate URLs on all 4 runs; the other nine show 0 of 4 in the same
    failure class. Regenerated from
    \texttt{data/results/error\_taxonomy.json}.}
  \label{tab:halluc}
\end{table}

\paragraph{Why.}
Table~\ref{tab:halluc} rules out both ``the LLM hallucinates'' and
``random noise'' narratives. Evidence:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
  \item Same LLM, different architectures diverge.
    \texttt{camel-ai-ds} (DeepSeek backend) has 0/4 halluc;
    \texttt{gpt-researcher-ds} (DeepSeek backend) has 4/4.
  \item Same architecture family, different LLMs diverge.
    \texttt{smolagents} (GLM-4.5) and \texttt{smolagents-ds}
    (DeepSeek) both 4/4 --- the architecture's
    pre-fix \texttt{</code>} / \texttt{<end\_code>} sentinel
    mismatch (fixed after the reported runs) dominates over LLM choice.
\end{itemize}
Probable causes per agent:
\begin{description}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
  \item[gpt-researcher-ds] RAG compiler fills the
    \texttt{\{sources\}} slot with \texttt{(placeholder)} strings when
    retrieval returns fewer sources than its minimum; the LLM then
    writes them out as if they were real URLs.
  \item[odr-ds] Summariser prompt treats source URLs as a soft
    constraint; DeepSeek sometimes paraphrases the URL into a
    plausible-looking but nonexistent one.
  \item[smolagents (both backends)] Pre-fix sentinel mismatch ---
    agent stops before any tool call, then writes a report entirely
    from training-memory and invents URLs to look grounded.
\end{description}

\paragraph{Takeaway.}
URL hallucination in deep-research agents is not a model-level property;
it is an engineering property of the agent's retrieval-composition
pipeline. This has practical implications: benchmark authors should
publish per-agent error-class distributions, not just a single composite;
and agent authors can fix hallucination by patching their pipeline
without upgrading the LLM.

\section{F3: IRT and Elo agree --- internal consistency}
\label{sec:irt}
%================================================================

\paragraph{Premise.}
Reviewers routinely ask whether a ranking produced by pairwise Elo is an
artefact of the Elo algorithm. We cross-check with a different
statistical framework: 2-parameter logistic Item Response Theory
\citep{lord-1980}.

\paragraph{Method.}
We dichotomise the $13 \times 4 = 52$ composite scores by per-task
median: $Y_{ij} = 1$ iff agent $i$'s composite on task $j$ is at or
above that task's median across the 13 agents. We fit
\begin{equation}
  P(Y_{ij} = 1 \mid \theta_i, a_j, b_j) =
  \sigma\big( a_j (\theta_i - b_j) \big)
  \label{eq:2pl}
\end{equation}
by joint MLE (L-BFGS-B) with a mean-zero, unit-variance soft penalty on
$\boldsymbol\theta$ for identifiability. The likelihood is invariant to
simultaneous sign flip of $\boldsymbol\theta$ and $\mathbf{a}$, so after
optimisation we flip both if
$\mathrm{corr}(\boldsymbol\theta, \bar Y_{i\cdot}) < 0$ to align with the
natural ``higher = better'' direction. Independently, we compute
Bradley--Terry Elo \citep{bradley-terry-1952} on all $\binom{13}{2}=78$
pairwise within-task wins derived from the composites.

\paragraph{Result.}
\begin{table}[h]
  \centering
  \small
  \input{tables/irt.tex}
  \caption{Agent ability under Bradley--Terry Elo and IRT 2-PL,
    regenerated from \texttt{data/results/arena\_elo\_ED.json} and
    \texttt{data/results/irt\_calibration.json}.
    Item parameters (not shown): all four tasks clip at the maximum
    discrimination bound $a_j = 5$ and cluster at $b_j \approx -0.33$.}
  \label{tab:irt}
\end{table}

\paragraph{Why.}
The two methods share no code path: Elo is a sequential
win-probability update from pairwise battles, while IRT is a global
MLE over a latent-trait model. That they produce the same ordering
is weak-but-independent evidence that the ranking signal in the data
is strong enough to be recovered under different assumptions.

The item parameters are revealing in a different way. All four tasks
peg $a_j = 5$ (our clipping bound), meaning each task is nearly a
perfect rank-recoverer for the 13 agents. All four also cluster at
$b_j \approx -0.33$, meaning they are approximately equally difficult.
This diagnoses a lack of \emph{task diversity}: our 4-task subset
measures a single ability dimension well but cannot distinguish
agents that specialise in, say, long-form synthesis from those that
specialise in short factoid recall. The scholarly extension
($n = 20$, distinct domains including medical, economic,
philosophical) is designed to introduce that diversity.

\paragraph{Takeaway.}
The ranking is not an Elo artefact. The 4-task consumer subset is
high-signal but low-diversity; longer subtests at different
$b$-values are necessary before this IRT fit can support quantitative
claims. We report IRT parameters as ``directional only'' until
the $13\times24$-cell matrix including scholarly tasks is complete.

\section{F4: Agent architecture, not retrieval, determines source diversity}
\label{sec:sources}
%================================================================

\paragraph{Premise.}
A standard simplifying assumption is that given identical retrieval
infrastructure, two agents see the same corpus and differ only in
downstream reasoning. We test this on the same sandbox shim,
fixing retrieval, and measuring which URLs surface in the final
report.

\paragraph{Method.}
Four scholarly tasks (\texttt{dr\_cross\_v3\_0088} common cold,
\texttt{0095} scientific method, \texttt{0100} inflation,
\texttt{0105} induced demand), each requiring cross-reference
between Wikipedia articles and forum discussions. The shim is patched
to index Kiwix (\texttt{localhost:8090}) alongside Magento and
Postmill; a representative \texttt{POST /search} query for ``common
cold rhinovirus virology'' returns six results of which three are
\texttt{localhost:8090} Wikipedia articles and three are shopping or
forum.

Two agents use the same shim URL and the same environment, differing
only in their internal composition:
\begin{description}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
  \item[\texttt{gpt5chat}] OpenAI Agents SDK tool-calling loop
    (\texttt{scripts/run\_gpt5.py}): the LLM reads a single consolidated
    search response, selects promising URLs, and calls
    \texttt{scrape(url)} individually.
  \item[\texttt{gpt-researcher}] Classical RAG-compilation: retrieved
    results are scored and re-ranked by a sub-LLM, then fed as
    compressed context into a writer LLM. The writer emits the final
    report and cites the compressed sources.
\end{description}

\paragraph{Result.}
\begin{table}[h]
  \centering
  \small
  \begin{tabular}{llrrrr}
    \toprule
    Agent & Architecture & Words & Cites & Wiki URLs & Forum URLs \\
    \midrule
    \multicolumn{6}{l}{\emph{Task 0088 (common cold)}} \\
    \texttt{gpt5chat}       & tool-call & 1111 & 7  & \textbf{2} & 1 \\
    \texttt{gpt-researcher} & RAG       & 1774 & 19 & \textbf{0} & --- \\
    \multicolumn{6}{l}{\emph{Task 0095 (scientific method)}} \\
    \texttt{gpt5chat}       & tool-call & 1354 & 11 & \textbf{3} & 0 \\
    \texttt{gpt-researcher} & RAG       & 1512 & 22 & \textbf{0} & --- \\
    \multicolumn{6}{l}{\emph{Task 0100 (inflation)}} \\
    \texttt{gpt5chat}       & tool-call & 2068 & 14 & \textbf{3} & 1 \\
    \texttt{gpt-researcher} & RAG       & 2363 & 13 & \textbf{0} & --- \\
    \multicolumn{6}{l}{\emph{Task 0105 (induced demand)}} \\
    \texttt{gpt5chat}       & tool-call & 1233 & 5  & \textbf{2} & 0 \\
    \texttt{gpt-researcher} & RAG       & 1792 & 9  & \textbf{0} & --- \\
    \midrule
    \emph{Mean}      \texttt{gpt5chat}       & & 1441 & 9.2  & \textbf{2.5} & 0.5 \\
    \emph{Mean}      \texttt{gpt-researcher} & & 1860 & 15.8 & \textbf{0.0} & --- \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
  \caption{Inline citations by host for two agents on four scholarly tasks,
    same shim, same sandbox. Wiki URL counts differ by an order of magnitude.}
  \label{tab:sources}
\end{table}

\paragraph{Why.}
The tool-calling agent processes each search result individually and
decides whether it is relevant to the task intent; short Wikipedia
snippets with clear topical match (``Common cold is a viral
infection\ldots'') rank equally with shopping PDPs in its decision.

The RAG-compilation agent first passes all retrieved items through a
relevance-ranker; that ranker --- tuned for consumer-research tasks
earlier in the pipeline --- rewards items containing hard numeric facts
(prices, ratings, review counts). Wikipedia articles with long-form prose
and few numerals score lower and are pruned before the writer LLM sees
them. The writer then produces a well-cited report consisting entirely
of surviving items: shopping pages and forum posts. No Wikipedia.

\paragraph{Takeaway.}
When reviewers ask ``did the agent retrieve the same sources?'' the
answer at the shim layer is yes, but at the composition layer is no.
Agent architecture is part of the information bottleneck, not just of
the reasoning step. This motivates a \texttt{source\_diversity} metric
(unique citation hosts, scaled by how many distinct hosts the shim
offered) orthogonal to citation quantity; under such a metric
\texttt{gpt5chat} outscores \texttt{gpt-researcher} on scholarly tasks
without needing more citations, differently from what the
existing \texttt{citation} pillar captures.

\section{Discussion}
%================================================================

The four findings are not equally mature:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{F1 (length)}: complete claim, presentable as a
    stand-alone sanity check with Table~\ref{tab:length}.
  \item \textbf{F2 (hallucination taxonomy)}: complete claim over the
    current 4-task set; upgrade to LLM-verified labels (as opposed to
    pattern-matching) would strengthen reviewer response on soft
    hallucination.
  \item \textbf{F3 (IRT)}: qualitative claim only; requires the
    $13\times 24$ scholarly-extended matrix before reporting
    quantitative $a,b$ parameters with confidence intervals.
  \item \textbf{F4 (source diversity)}: $n=2$ agents, $n=4$ tasks ---
    initial observation, not yet a claim. The ablation requires the
    same measurement across all 13 agents once scholarly runs complete.
\end{itemize}

All four findings share a structural property: they are only visible
because the benchmark decomposes the score into multiple pillars and
tracks failure modes beyond success/failure. A single-score live-web
benchmark could not have surfaced any of them: length-bias testing
requires per-pillar correlations (F1); hallucination detection
requires offline URL enumeration against a known sandbox (F2);
IRT cross-validation requires a stable, frozen-corpus matrix (F3);
source-diversity ablation requires full URL provenance per report (F4).
