
Analysis: 'The scientific method — canonical vs. practised'

(1) Wikipedia facts:

- Falsification: Karl Popper argued that scientific theories must be falsifiable; they cannot be proven true, only withstand attempts at falsification (Popper, 1934). [Source: Wikipedia 'Philosophy of science']
- Paradigm shifts: Thomas Kuhn introduced the concept of paradigm shifts, where normal science operates within a dominant paradigm until anomalies accumulate, leading to a revolutionary shift to a new paradigm (Kuhn, 1962). [Source: Wikipedia 'Thomas Kuhn']
- Reproducibility: A key principle of the scientific method is that experiments must be reproducible by other researchers to verify results. [Source: Wikipedia 'Scientific method']
- Falsification in practice: Popper's falsificationism is often contrasted with verificationism; he emphasized that scientists should attempt to disprove hypotheses rather than confirm them. [Source: Wikipedia 'Philosophy of science']
- Paradigm example: The shift from Newtonian mechanics to Einstein's relativity is a classic example of a paradigm shift. [Source: Wikipedia 'Thomas Kuhn']
- Reproducibility crisis: Many scientific fields face a reproducibility crisis where published results cannot be replicated, raising questions about the robustness of scientific practice. [Source: Wikipedia 'Reproducibility']

Short commentary on the replication crisis:

The replication crisis highlights a gap between canonical scientific method and practised science. While the method emphasizes reproducibility as a cornerstone, in practice, factors like p-hacking, selective reporting, and pressure to publish novel results lead to many findings that cannot be replicated. This crisis has spurred reforms such as preregistration, open data, and larger sample sizes. It underscores the need for the scientific community to align practice with principles to maintain trust in scientific knowledge.
