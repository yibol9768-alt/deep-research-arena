# Policy-Informed Consumer Guide: What Actually Works for the Common Cold

## 1. Wikipedia Facts
- The common cold is primarily caused by rhinoviruses (50-80% of cases) and also by coronaviruses, adenoviruses, and others. [Wikipedia: Common cold]
- Symptoms include sore throat, runny nose, coughing, sneezing, and fatigue, usually lasting 7-10 days. [Wikipedia: Common cold]
- There is no cure; treatment focuses on symptom relief. Evidence supports zinc lozenges may reduce duration if taken within 24 hours of symptom onset. [Wikipedia: Common cold]
- Over-the-counter (OTC) drugs for colds are regulated by the FDA in the US under the OTC Drug Review process established in 1972. [Wikipedia: Over-the-counter drug]
- Common OTC ingredients like dextromethorphan (cough suppressant) and pseudoephedrine (decongestant) have evidence of efficacy but also side effects. [Wikipedia: Over-the-counter drug]

## 3. Evidence-Backed Remedies vs. Community Folklore
### Evidence-Backed (from Wikipedia and scientific literature):
- Zinc lozenges (reduce duration if taken early) [Wikipedia]
- Rest and hydration (supportive care) [Wikipedia]
- Nasal decongestants (pseudoephedrine) for symptom relief [Wikipedia]
### Community Folklore (from Reddit reports):
- Chicken soup (may provide comfort but no antiviral effect)
- Mega-dose vitamin C (no consistent evidence)
- Elderberry syrup (limited evidence)

## 4. Common Misconceptions
1. **Misconception**: Antibiotics can treat the common cold. **Fact**: Colds are viral; antibiotics are ineffective and contribute to resistance. [Wikipedia]
2. **Misconception**: Vitamin C prevents colds. **Fact**: Regular supplementation may slightly reduce duration but does not prevent infection in the general population. [Wikipedia]

## 5. Policy Implications
OTC cold medications are widely used but vary in efficacy. Regulatory history shows ongoing evaluation of safety and efficacy. Consumers should rely on evidence-based interventions and consult healthcare providers for persistent symptoms.
