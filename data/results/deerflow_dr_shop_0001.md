## Survey Note

### Literature Review & Theoretical Framework

The evaluation of consumer products via aggregated star ratings has been extensively studied in e-commerce research. Established literature demonstrates that rating reliability is a direct function of review volume: Chevalier and Mayzlin (2006) established that consumer purchasing decisions are significantly influenced by both average rating and review count, while Filieri et al. (2015) noted that consumers perceive ratings supported by greater review volume as more trustworthy. The present analysis applies these principles by weighting review volume as a co-equal dimension alongside raw rating scores, thereby avoiding the well-documented "perfect score bias" wherein products with minimal reviews appear superior to those with more established track records.

### Methodology & Data Analysis

The research methodology comprised four sequential phases: (1) systematic product discovery via multiple search query formulations, (2) threshold screening at ≥4.0 stars, (3) individual product page verification to confirm all data points, and (4) multi-dimensional comparative evaluation. A notable methodological constraint is the absence of standardized categorization on the platform — products may appear under different search terms depending on their listing metadata — which necessitated the use of four distinct query formulations to maximize discovery coverage. All data was collected from the One Stop Market platform at `localhost:7770` and verified at the individual product page level.

### Critical Discussion

Several limitations warrant acknowledgment. First, the review volumes across the dataset are generally low (1–12 reviews), which constrains the statistical power of any rating-based inference. The distinction between a 4.2 and 4.3 average rating, for instance, is not statistically significant at these sample sizes. Second, the platform's review system does not appear to differentiate verified purchases from unverified submissions, introducing potential noise into the rating data. Third, the "Renewed" designation of the Edifier H840 introduces a confounding variable, as consumer expectations and evaluation criteria may differ systematically for refurbished products. Fourth, price data represents a single snapshot and may not reflect temporal fluctuations or promotional pricing.

### Future Research Directions

Subsequent analyses would benefit from: (1) longitudinal monitoring of rating evolution as review volumes increase; (2) sentiment analysis of individual review text to complement aggregate star ratings; (3) comparison of One Stop Market pricing against competing platforms to assess competitive positioning; and (4) incorporation of product specification data (driver size, frequency response, impedance) to enable technical performance benchmarking alongside consumer satisfaction metrics.
