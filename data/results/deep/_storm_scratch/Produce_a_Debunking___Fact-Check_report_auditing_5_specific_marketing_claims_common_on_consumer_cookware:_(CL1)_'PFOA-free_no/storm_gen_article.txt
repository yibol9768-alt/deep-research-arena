# Debunking common consumer cookware marketing claims

## CL1: 'PFOA-free non-stick is safe at all temperatures'

The marketing claim that PFOA-free non-stick cookware is safe at all temperatures is misleading. While modern non-stick pans use PTFE (polytetrafluoroethylene) coatings, such as Teflon, which are manufactured without PFOA (perfluorooctanoic acid), the PTFE coating itself still has temperature limitations. PTFE was invented by Roy Plunkett in 1938 and is known for its corrosion resistance and non-stick properties[1][2]. However, when PTFE is heated above 500°F (260°C), it begins to decompose and release potentially toxic fumes. This means that even PFOA-free non-stick pans should not be used for high-heat cooking methods such as searing or broiling. The safety of the coating is dependent on temperature control, not merely the absence of PFOA.

## CL2: 'Ceramic-coated pans last as long as PTFE pans'

The claim that ceramic-coated pans have comparable longevity to PTFE (Teflon)-coated pans is not supported by typical consumer experience. Ceramic coatings are generally less durable than PTFE coatings. While PTFE-based non-stick surfaces, such as those made from Teflon, are known for their chemical inertness and resistance to corrosion[1][2], ceramic coatings are more prone to scratching, chipping, and loss of non-stick properties over time. Ceramic coatings are typically made from a sol-gel process that creates a glass-like surface, which is harder but more brittle than PTFE. This brittleness makes ceramic pans more susceptible to damage from metal utensils, thermal shock, and everyday wear, resulting in a shorter effective lifespan compared to quality PTFE pans under normal use.

## CL3: 'Cast iron skillets contribute meaningful dietary iron'

The claim that cast iron skillets contribute meaningful amounts of dietary iron is partially true but often overstated. Cooking with cast iron can indeed increase the iron content of food, particularly when cooking acidic foods like tomato sauce, which leach more iron from the pan. However, the amount of iron transferred varies significantly based on cooking time, food acidity, and the seasoning of the pan. Historical use of cast iron was common in the American colonies, and improvements in metallurgy allowed for more widespread production of iron cookware[3]. While the additional iron can be beneficial for individuals with iron deficiency, it is not a reliable or significant source for most people, and the amounts are generally small compared to dietary supplements or iron-rich foods.

## CL4: 'Stainless steel is completely non-reactive'

The claim that stainless steel is completely non-reactive is inaccurate. While stainless steel is highly resistant to corrosion and reactions with most foods, it is not entirely inert. Stainless steel cookware can leach small amounts of nickel and chromium into food, particularly when cooking acidic foods for extended periods or at high temperatures. The alloy composition of stainless steel (typically including iron, chromium, nickel, and other metals) means that under certain conditions, especially with prolonged contact with acidic ingredients like tomato sauce or vinegar, trace amounts of these metals can migrate into the food. For most people, this is not a health concern, but individuals with nickel allergies may need to be cautious. Additionally, stainless steel is not as non-stick as PTFE-coated pans and can cause food to stick if not properly preheated or oiled.

## CL5: 'All non-stick coatings are the same'

The claim that all non-stick coatings are the same is false. Non-stick cookware coatings vary significantly in composition, performance, and safety. The most common type is PTFE (polytetrafluoroethylene), marketed as Teflon, which is a fluoropolymer that provides excellent non-stick properties and chemical resistance[1][2]. In contrast, ceramic coatings are made from inorganic materials like silicon dioxide and are free of fluoropolymers. Ceramic coatings generally offer lower non-stick performance and durability compared to PTFE but are marketed as more environmentally friendly. Other types include enameled coatings and seasoned cast iron surfaces. Each type has different heat tolerances, longevity, and care requirements. Consumers should not assume that all non-stick pans perform identically, as the choice involves trade-offs between ease of use, durability, and safety.
