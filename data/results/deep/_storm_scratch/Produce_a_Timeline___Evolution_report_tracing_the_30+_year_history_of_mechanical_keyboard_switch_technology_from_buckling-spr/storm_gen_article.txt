# Timeline of Mechanical Keyboard Switch Technology

## 1980s: The Buckling-Spring Era

The history of modern mechanical keyboard switches begins with IBM's buckling-spring mechanism. Introduced with the original IBM PC in August 1981, the **IBM Model F** keyboard featured a capacitive buckling-spring design that registered key presses through changes in capacitance when the spring buckled[1]. This design was electronically incompatible with later IBM keyboard types but established the foundation for tactile mechanical typing[1].
The **IBM Model M**, introduced in the mid-1980s, refined the buckling-spring concept by using a membrane-based registration system beneath the springs. Produced by IBM, Lexmark, and later Unicomp, the Model M became one of the most iconic keyboards in computing history, featuring 101/102/104-key ANSI layouts and 102/103/105/122-key ISO variants[2]. Keycaps on these early keyboards were often manufactured with integrated keyswitch and keycap units, though later models adopted separate keycaps that could be removed with a tool[3]. The buckling-spring design provided a distinctive tactile and audible feedback that remains highly sought after by enthusiasts decades later[2].

## 1990s–2010s: Cherry MX Dominance

The 1990s marked the rise of **Cherry MX switches**, which would come to dominate the mechanical keyboard market for over two decades. German manufacturer Cherry developed the MX switch family, featuring a cross-shaped stem that accepted standard keycaps and offered multiple color-coded variants for different tactile and auditory preferences. The Cherry MX design became the de facto standard for mechanical keyboards, used in everything from industrial terminals to gaming peripherals[4].
During this period, the personal computer market expanded dramatically, and mechanical keyboards were increasingly marketed to consumers as affordable and accessible devices for single, non-technical users[5]. The **IBM Personal System/2** line, including the Model 70 (released 1988), continued to use buckling-spring keyboards but gradually gave way to Cherry MX-equipped boards in the broader market[6]. By the 2000s and 2010s, Cherry MX switches—particularly the Blue (clicky), Brown (tactile), and Red (linear) variants—had become synonymous with mechanical keyboard performance[7][8][9].
The 2010s saw a resurgence of interest in mechanical keyboards among gamers and typists, leading to the introduction of competing switch manufacturers such as **Gateron** and **Kailh**. Gateron switches, often praised for their smoothness, became popular in budget-friendly and enthusiast boards, such as the EPOMAKER AKKO 3061S (Gateron Yellow switches)[10] and the Keychron C2 (Gateron G Pro Blue switches)[11]. Cherry MX remained the premium choice, featured in products like the Code V3 with Cherry MX Brown switches[7] and the Mistel X-VIII with Cherry MX Silent Red switches[8].

## 2020s: Hot-Swap, Optical, and Magnetic-Hall Switches

The 2020s introduced a new era of innovation, driven by demands for customization, speed, and durability. **Hot-swappable sockets** became a standard feature, allowing users to replace switches without soldering. Keyboards like the EPOMAKER AKKO 3061S[10], Durgod Hades 68[9], and Keychron C2[11] offered hot-swap capabilities, supporting both traditional mechanical switches and newer optical variants.
**Optical switches** emerged as a major alternative, using light beams to register key presses instead of metal contacts. This design offered faster response times and greater durability, as there were no physical contacts to wear out. Products like the Guffercty kred SK61 utilized Gateron Optical Yellow switches, boasting hot-swappable functionality and RGB backlighting[12].
**Magnetic-Hall effect switches** represented the next frontier, using magnets and Hall effect sensors to detect key position. The **SteelSeries Apex Pro** (priced at $149.99) pioneered adjustable actuation points, allowing users to set the exact distance at which a key press registered[13]. This technology enabled dynamic actuation profiles—for example, setting WASD keys to shallow actuation for gaming while keeping other keys at full travel for typing. The SteelSeries Apex Pro was marketed as "the world's fastest mechanical keyboard" due to its adjustable actuation switches[13].
The 2020s also saw the proliferation of **smaller form factors**, particularly 60% keyboards (such as the EPOMAKER AKKO 3061S[10] and Guffercty kred SK61[12]), 65% layouts (Durgod Hades 68[9]), and 87-key tenkeyless designs (Code V3[7]). These compact layouts, combined with hot-swap sockets and programmable firmware, allowed users to customize every aspect of their typing experience. The market also expanded to include wearable and stickable Bluetooth remotes like the Chubby Buttons 2[14], demonstrating the diversification of input device technology beyond traditional keyboards.
The evolution from buckling-spring to Cherry MX to modern hot-swap, optical, and magnetic-Hall switches reflects a continuous drive toward greater customization, faster actuation, and longer service life, with each generation building upon the innovations of its predecessors[4].
