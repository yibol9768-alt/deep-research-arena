Software updates for Matter-compatible hubs began rolling out in late 2022, with Matter-enabled devices entering the market in 2023[1].
Other protocols such as Zigbee and Z-Wave, while related to Matter, have distinct security models. # Overview of Security Models

The security models employed by smart-home wireless protocols can be broadly categorized by their approach to authentication, encryption, key management, and resistance to common attack vectors. These models directly influence the threat surface of devices such as smart locks, cameras, and hubs, and are critical for understanding the overall security posture of a smart-home ecosystem.

## Centralized vs. Decentralized Trust Models

Protocols like **Matter** (the Connectivity Standards Alliance standard for smart home interoperability) adopt a centralized trust model based on a Public Key Infrastructure (PKI) and device attestation certificates[1]. In this model, each device is provisioned with a unique certificate signed by a trusted authority, and communication is only permitted after mutual authentication. This approach provides strong identity verification and resistance to device impersonation attacks. However, the reliance on a central certificate authority introduces a single point of failure; if the root key is compromised, all devices in the ecosystem may be vulnerable.
In contrast, protocols such as **Zigbee** and **Z-Wave** historically relied on decentralized or pre-shared key models. For example, early Zigbee implementations used a network-wide pre-shared key, which, if extracted from one device, could compromise the entire network[2]. Later revisions, including the Zigbee 3.0 protocol, introduced countermeasures against attacks such as removing already paired devices from the network to listen to key exchanges during re-pairing[2]. This evolution reflects an ongoing effort to move toward more robust key management and session-specific encryption.

## Encryption and Data Protection

All major smart-home protocols employ encryption for over-the-air communication. **Wi-Fi** (used in many cameras and hubs) typically relies on WPA2 or WPA3 for link-layer encryption, while **Thread** and **Matter** mandate the use of modern cryptographic suites, including AES-128 or AES-256, depending on the implementation[1]. **Z-Wave** uses a proprietary encryption scheme, while **Zigbee** employs AES-128 with a CCM* mode. The strength of encryption is a key factor in the threat surface, as weaker or improperly implemented ciphers can be vulnerable to eavesdropping or replay attacks.

## Algorithmic and Social Biases in Security Models

The security model of a smart-home protocol extends beyond cryptography to include the algorithms that govern data collection, user profiling, and decision-making. **Algorithmic bias** has been observed in search engines and social media platforms, where unintended or unanticipated decisions arise from the way data is coded, collected, or selected to train algorithms[3][8]. In the context of smart-home security cameras, such biases can manifest in motion detection algorithms that disproportionately misidentify certain individuals or fail to detect threats based on flawed training data. This creates a **filter bubble** effect, where the system's responses are based on incomplete or skewed data about the user's environment, potentially leading to false negatives or privacy violations[8]. These algorithmic biases represent a distinct threat surface that is not addressed by encryption alone.

## Physical and Social Threat Surfaces

Security models must also account for physical and social threats. Dummy cameras, such as the "4Pcs Dome Simulation Dummy Fake Security Camera," rely on a purely deterrent-based model with no actual encryption or monitoring capabilities[9]. Their threat surface is limited to social engineering (e.g., a savvy intruder recognizing the lack of real components). In contrast, systems like **Deep Sentinel** combine hardware cameras with live guard monitoring, introducing a human-in-the-loop model that adds a layer of social verification but also creates a privacy risk if the monitoring service is compromised[6]. The **Lorex E892AB-E** camera, rated 5.0/5, uses "Smart Motion Detection Plus," which incorporates algorithmic analysis to differentiate between humans, animals, and vehicles, reducing false alarms but also relying on the integrity of the underlying algorithm[7].

## Interoperability and Protocol Stack Security

The **Matter** standard is designed to improve interoperability across protocols such as Zigbee, Z-Wave, Thread, and Wi-Fi, but this interconnectivity also expands the attack surface[1]. A vulnerability in one protocol's implementation could be exploited to gain access to devices on another network through a Matter hub. The security model must therefore consider not only the individual protocol but also the **hub's** role as a bridge between different security domains. The Matter specification includes device attestation and mandatory encryption, but the practical security depends on the correct implementation of these features across all participating manufacturers.

# Threat Surface Classification

## Communication Channel Threats

Smart-home wireless protocols expose multiple threat surfaces through their communication channels. The most fundamental vulnerability is **eavesdropping**, where an attacker passively monitors wireless transmissions to capture unencrypted data or metadata. Protocols operating in the 2.4 GHz ISM band, such as Zigbee, Z-Wave, Thread, and Wi-Fi, are particularly susceptible to signal interception due to the ubiquity of software-defined radios capable of demodulating these frequencies[2][4]. Bluetooth-based implementations, including Bluetooth 4.1 found in some smart home devices[10], face similar risks, though Bluetooth 3.0 introduced countermeasures against passive key-exchange interception during device re-pairing[2].
**Man-in-the-middle (MITM) attacks** represent a critical threat, where an attacker intercepts and potentially modifies communications between devices. The Matter standard attempts to mitigate this through mandatory use of public key infrastructure (PKI) and device attestation certificates, ensuring that only authenticated devices can participate in the network[1]. However, legacy protocols without such cryptographic guarantees remain vulnerable.
**Replay attacks** occur when captured legitimate transmissions are retransmitted to trigger unauthorized actions, such as unlocking a smart lock or disabling a security camera. The effectiveness of this attack varies by protocol; Zigbee networks simulated in tools like ns-2 and OMNeT++ demonstrate that protocol-level sequence numbering can provide some protection, but implementations must be verified[2].

## Device Authentication and Authorization Threats

**Spoofing** involves an attacker impersonating a legitimate device to gain network access or issue commands. The Apple Home platform, using the HomeKit framework, addresses this through hardware-backed authentication and secure pairing[11]. In contrast, simpler devices such as fingerprint readers[12] and encrypted USB flash drives[13][14] rely on local authentication methods that may not extend to network-level verification.
**Unauthorized pairing** occurs when an attacker forces a device to associate with a rogue controller. The Matter protocol's certification process and use of device attestation certificates are designed to prevent this[1]. However, devices using older protocols like DLNA (Digital Living Network Alliance) lack robust pairing mechanisms and may accept connections from any compatible controller on the same network[15].
**Key extraction** threats affect devices where cryptographic keys are stored in accessible hardware. The RP2040 microcontroller, used in some smart home controllers, stores keys in flash memory that could potentially be read through physical access or debug interfaces[16]. Hardware-encrypted storage solutions like the Aegis Secure Key 3 employ FIPS 140-3 Level 3 validated encryption to resist such extraction attempts[13].

## Physical Layer Threats

**Jamming** attacks disrupt wireless communications by transmitting noise on the same frequency band. The 2.4 GHz band, shared by Wi-Fi, Zigbee, Z-Wave, and Bluetooth devices, is particularly vulnerable to wideband jammers that can simultaneously disrupt multiple protocols[4]. This can disable security cameras, prevent smart locks from receiving commands, or force devices to fall back to less secure modes.
**Tampering** involves physical modification of devices to extract cryptographic material, install malicious firmware, or bypass security controls. Smart locks with physical key override mechanisms[5] and fingerprint readers with exposed serial communication interfaces[12] present accessible tamper points. The SH-CHEN Fpm10A fingerprint module, for instance, communicates via serial interface, which could be probed if the device enclosure is compromised[12].
**Side-channel attacks** exploit physical characteristics such as power consumption, electromagnetic emissions, or timing variations to extract sensitive information. While less common in consumer smart home devices, the cryptographic operations performed by hardware-encrypted USB drives[13] and secure elements in smart hubs could theoretically be vulnerable to such attacks.

## Network Infrastructure Threats

**Router and hub compromise** represents a high-severity threat, as these devices control all smart home communications. The Matter standard's reliance on existing Wi-Fi and Thread networks means that a compromised hub could bypass Matter's security features entirely[1]. Similarly, Wi-Fi range extenders and repeaters[17][18][19] that lack proper security updates become network entry points for attackers.
**DNS and routing manipulation** can redirect smart home devices to malicious servers or intercept firmware updates. The HTTP protocol example[20] illustrates how basic web requests could be intercepted if not properly secured with TLS. Devices that rely on cloud services, such as the NU-Set Eyecon smart lock box[5], are particularly vulnerable to DNS spoofing attacks.
**Firmware update interception** allows attackers to install malicious code on smart home devices. The Matter protocol mandates signed firmware updates, but older devices may accept unsigned updates over unencrypted channels[1]. The DLNA standard's lack of mandatory update verification[15] makes DLNA-certified devices potential targets for firmware-based attacks.

## Data Privacy Threats

**Behavioral profiling** occurs when smart home devices leak usage patterns through metadata. Smart locks that report unlock times[5], cameras that stream video to cloud services[7], and motion sensors that transmit detection events all create data trails that could be exploited for surveillance or social engineering.
**Algorithmic bias** in smart home systems can lead to discriminatory outcomes, particularly in security applications such as facial recognition cameras or access control systems. The collected information on algorithmic bias[3] notes that bias can arise from data collection and training methods, affecting privacy and reinforcing social biases. This is relevant to smart cameras that use machine learning for threat detection.
**Filter bubbles** created by personalized smart home automation[8] can lead to security blind spots, where users are not exposed to information about alternative security configurations or threat warnings. This intellectual isolation may cause users to remain unaware of vulnerabilities in their specific device configurations.

## Supply Chain and Ecosystem Threats

**Counterfeit devices** that mimic legitimate smart home products may contain backdoors or reduced security implementations. Fake security cameras with flashing LEDs[9][21] are examples of devices that provide no actual surveillance functionality while appearing legitimate. Such devices can be used to create false sense of security or as entry points to home networks.
**Interoperability vulnerabilities** arise when devices from different manufacturers communicate using the same protocol but implement security inconsistently. The Matter standard aims to address this through certification[1], but the sheer variety of devices—from fingerprint readers[12] to smart speakers[22] to gaming PCs[23]—means that security profiles vary widely even within the same protocol family.
**End-of-life threats** affect devices that no longer receive security updates. Older smart home hubs and controllers, such as those running Android 9.0 Pie[10], may have unpatched vulnerabilities that attackers can exploit. The 2023 release of Matter-compatible devices[1] creates a bifurcated ecosystem where newer devices have stronger security postures than legacy devices.

# Protocol Catalog by Security Profile

## High-Risk Protocols (Weak Encryption or Known Vulnerabilities)

### Zigbee (IEEE 802.15.4-based)

Zigbee is a low-power, mesh-networking protocol widely used in smart locks, bulbs (e.g., Philips Hue[24]), and sensors. Security relies on a symmetric key model using a network key and a link key, both of which can be captured during over-the-air joining if not properly implemented. The protocol's security profile includes AES-128 encryption, but the key exchange process has historically been vulnerable to attacks such as network key extraction from unencrypted beacons. The Zigbee 3.0 specification introduced countermeasures against re-pairing attacks, where an adversary removes a paired device to eavesdrop on the key exchange[2]. However, many deployed devices still use older stacks without these fixes, making them susceptible to deauthentication attacks and key compromise. Zigbee networks lack per-device public key infrastructure (PKI) unless integrated with a hub that provides certificate-based authentication.

### Z-Wave

Z-Wave operates in the sub-GHz ISM band (e.g., 908.42 MHz in the US) and uses AES-128 encryption in its S2 security class. While S2 mandates inclusion with a PIN or QR code to prevent unauthorized joining, older S0 devices are vulnerable to replay attacks and key sniffing. The protocol's centralized topology (controller-based) means that compromising the hub exposes all connected devices. Z-Wave's security profile is rated as moderate, but legacy devices without S2 certification remain a significant threat surface in mixed-protocol deployments[1].

### Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE)

BLE is used in many smart locks (e.g., NU-Set Eyecon[5]) and proximity trackers (e.g., AirTag mounts[25]). BLE's security relies on the Secure Connections feature with Elliptic Curve Diffie-Hellman (ECDH) key exchange, but many devices implement only the legacy "Just Works" pairing method, which is vulnerable to passive eavesdropping and man-in-the-middle attacks. The BLE 4.0/4.2 protocols lack the enhanced privacy features of later versions, and devices that do not enforce MITM protection can have their encryption keys captured during re-pairing. BLE's range of up to 100 meters makes it susceptible to long-distance sniffing with directional antennas.

## Medium-Risk Protocols (Adequate Encryption but Implementation Gaps)

### Wi-Fi (IEEE 802.11 a/b/g/n/ac/ax)

Wi-Fi is ubiquitous in smart cameras (e.g., Lorex 4K systems[26]) and hubs.
- **Weak passphrases**: Many devices default to WPA2-PSK with easily guessable credentials.
- **Firmware vulnerabilities**: IP cameras often run outdated Linux kernels with known exploits.
- **Spectrum limitations**: The 2.4 GHz band only permits 11 channels in the US, leading to congestion and potential jamming[4].
Wi-Fi devices are typically exposed to the internet via cloud services, creating a larger attack surface than local-only protocols. The protocol itself is secure when properly configured (WPA3-Enterprise), but consumer-grade implementations frequently fail to enforce strong authentication.

### Thread

Thread is an IPv6-based mesh protocol designed for low-power IoT devices, using AES-128 encryption and a commissioning process similar to Zigbee. Its security model includes device authentication via PKI certificates (distributed during commissioning), but the protocol's relative newness means fewer security audits exist. Thread networks rely on a Border Router for internet connectivity, which becomes a single point of compromise. The protocol is considered medium-risk due to its strong cryptographic foundations but limited real-world attack surface analysis.

## Low-Risk Protocols (Strong Security by Design)

### Matter

Matter is an application-layer standard that operates over Thread, Wi-Fi, or Ethernet, and mandates a robust security model from the outset.
- **Device attestation**: Each device carries a unique Device Attestation Certificate (DAC) signed by the Connectivity Standards Alliance (CSA), verified during commissioning to prevent counterfeit hardware.
- **Public Key Infrastructure (PKI)**: All communications are encrypted using ECDHE (Elliptic Curve Diffie-Hellman Ephemeral) key exchange, ensuring forward secrecy.
- **Secure commissioning**: Devices can only be added to a Matter fabric (network) by an authorized controller using a passcode or QR code, with no legacy fallback to weaker modes.
- **Certificate revocation**: The CSA maintains a Certificate Revocation List (CRL) for compromised certificates.
Matter code and documentation are published under the Apache License, allowing open-source security auditing[1]. The protocol's first stable release (1.0) in October 2022, with subsequent updates (latest 1.5.0.1 as of December 2025), includes security patches. Matter's security profile is the most robust among smart home protocols, though its reliance on cloud-based certificate validation introduces a dependency on CSA infrastructure.

### Apple HomeKit (with Matter)

Apple HomeKit, when configured with Matter devices, inherits Matter's PKI-based security. HomeKit's own protocol (for non-Matter accessories) uses end-to-end encryption with HomeKit Accessory Protocol (HAP), requiring each accessory to have a unique pairing identity and encryption key. Siri voice commands and automations are processed locally on the Apple hub (Apple TV, HomePod, or iPad), minimizing cloud exposure[11]. HomeKit's security profile is rated low-risk due to mandatory hardware encryption chips and regular security audits, but it is limited to Apple's ecosystem.

# Comparative Security Profiles

## Matter

Matter is a smart home connectivity standard published by the Connectivity Standards Alliance (CSA) that aims to improve interoperability and compatibility among Internet of Things (IoT) devices[1]. From a security perspective, Matter incorporates a public key infrastructure (PKI) and device attestation certificates as core security mechanisms[1]. These attestation certificates ensure that only certified, trusted devices can join a Matter network, providing a robust foundation against unauthorized device injection. The standard's use of Internet Protocol (IP) as its base standard, alongside related standards such as Zigbee, Z-Wave, Thread, and Wi-Fi, means its security model must account for the threat surfaces of multiple underlying transport layers[1]. Matter-compatible software updates began rolling out in late 2022, with device releases following in 2023, making its security profile relatively modern and designed with current cryptographic best practices[1]. However, because Matter relies on existing wireless protocols for physical and link-layer communication, its overall threat surface is partially inherited from those protocols. For example, if a Matter device communicates over Wi-Fi, it is susceptible to Wi-Fi-specific attacks such as deauthentication or rogue access points, unless the Matter application layer provides compensating controls.

## Zigbee

Zigbee is a wireless protocol built on the IEEE 802.15.4 standard, commonly used in smart home devices including locks and sensors[2]. The Zigbee 3.0 protocol features specific security countermeasures against attacks that involve removing already paired devices from the network to intercept key exchanges during re-pairing[2]. This is a significant threat surface: an attacker who physically or wirelessly deauthorizes a device could attempt to capture the network key when the device rejoins. Zigbee's security model relies on symmetric key cryptography, with a network key shared among all devices and a link key used for device-to-device communication. The threat surface includes potential key extraction from compromised devices, replay attacks, and denial-of-service via signal jamming on the 2.4 GHz ISM band, which is shared with Wi-Fi and Bluetooth[4]. Network simulators such as ns-2, OMNeT++, OPNET, and NetSim are available to model Zigbee networks, allowing researchers to analyze attack vectors and defense mechanisms in controlled environments[2].

## Wi-Fi (IEEE 802.11)

Wi-Fi, operating in the 2.4 GHz and 5 GHz bands, is a common transport for smart home cameras, locks, and hubs[4]. Its security model is primarily defined by the Wi-Fi Protected Access (WPA) family of protocols, with WPA3 offering the strongest protections against offline dictionary attacks and forward secrecy. However, Wi-Fi's threat surface is broad: spectrum assignments vary by region, with the United States permitting 11 channels in the 2.4 GHz band, while Australia and Europe allow 13, and Japan allows 14[4]. This regulatory fragmentation can create configuration vulnerabilities if devices are not properly region-locked. Additionally, shared spectrum with Bluetooth, Zigbee, and USB 3.0 hubs introduces interference and potential side-channel risks[4]. For smart locks and cameras, Wi-Fi's reliance on a central access point creates a single point of failure; compromise of the router or AP can expose all connected IoT traffic.

## Bluetooth and Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE)

Bluetooth, including BLE, is frequently used in smart locks for proximity-based unlocking and key sharing. Its security model includes pairing protocols (e.g., Just Works, Passkey Entry, and Numeric Comparison) with varying levels of protection against eavesdropping and man-in-the-middle attacks. The threat surface for BLE in smart locks is particularly concerning due to the short-range nature of the protocol, which can allow attackers to scan for discoverable devices from up to 100 meters with directional antennas. The NU-Set Eyecon WiFi-Enabled Smart Lock Box, which features a "Blockchain Secure Photo Key," exemplifies a hybrid approach, combining BLE for local communication with Wi-Fi for remote access, thereby expanding the attack surface to both wireless protocols[5].

## Proprietary and Hybrid Protocols

Several commercial smart home devices employ proprietary or hybrid wireless protocols. For instance, the Lorex E892AB-E 8MP 4K Ultra HD Smart Deterrence IP Bullet Security Camera uses "Smart Motion Detection Plus" over an IP network, likely combining Wi-Fi or Ethernet with proprietary motion-sensing algorithms[7]. The Deep Sentinel system, which includes three night vision cameras and a smart hub, integrates real professional guard monitoring, implying a cloud-based security model where video data is transmitted over the internet, introducing threats related to data interception, cloud server compromise, and man-in-the-middle attacks on the video stream[6]. The RP2040-Plus MCU board, based on the Raspberry Pi RP2040 chip, is an example of a development platform that could be used in custom smart home hubs, with its dual-core ARM Cortex M0+ processor and USB-C connector representing a physical and USB-based attack vector[16]. The Apricorn Aegis Secure Key 3 NXC, a hardware-encrypted USB flash drive with FIPS 140-3 Level 3 validation, illustrates a high-security storage device that could be used to securely store cryptographic keys for smart home hubs, but its USB interface remains a potential entry point for malware[13].

## Threat Surface Summary

The threat surfaces across these protocols can be categorized as follows:
- **Network-layer attacks**: Eavesdropping, replay, man-in-the-middle, and denial-of-service, applicable to Wi-Fi, Zigbee, BLE, and Matter.
- **Physical-layer attacks**: Jamming on shared ISM bands (2.4 GHz and 5 GHz), affecting all wireless protocols[4].
- **Device attestation vulnerabilities**: Matter's PKI mitigates counterfeit devices, but legacy protocols like Zigbee and BLE may lack such robust attestation[1].
- **Key management weaknesses**: Zigbee's re-pairing attack vector and BLE's weak pairing modes are documented threats[2].
- **Cloud and remote access risks**: Devices like the Deep Sentinel and Lorex cameras that rely on cloud services introduce threats related to data privacy, account hijacking, and insecure APIs[6][7].
- **Physical and USB interfaces**: Hubs and development boards (e.g., RP2040-Plus) expose USB ports that can be used for direct data extraction or firmware injection[16].

# Recommendations for Security Researchers

## Protocol Selection and Analysis Priorities

Security researchers studying smart-home ecosystems should prioritize protocols that exhibit the widest attack surface and most complex security models. **Zigbee** remains a high-value target due to its mesh networking topology, which introduces multiple points of potential compromise, including device pairing mechanisms, key exchange procedures, and routing table manipulation[2]. Researchers should focus on Zigbee 3.0's countermeasures against re-pairing attacks, where an adversary removes a paired device to eavesdrop on key exchange[2]. The protocol's simulation environments (ns-2, OMNeT++, OPNET, NetSim) provide accessible platforms for vulnerability testing without requiring physical hardware[2].
**Matter**, as the newer interoperability standard, presents unique research opportunities around its certificate-based security model. The standard's reliance on public key infrastructure (PKI) and device attestation certificates creates a trust chain that researchers should examine for implementation flaws across different vendors[1]. Since Matter aims to bridge multiple protocols (Zigbee, Z-Wave, Thread, Wi-Fi), researchers should investigate how security properties are translated or potentially weakened when commands traverse protocol boundaries[1]. The open-source codebase (Apache License) allows for static analysis and fuzzing of the core implementation[1].

## Hardware Attack Surface Evaluation

Researchers should acquire representative devices from each protocol ecosystem for hands-on testing. The **Lorex E892AB-E** (8MP 4K IP bullet camera, $139.95) provides a high-resolution IP camera target with smart motion detection, enabling analysis of video stream encryption, cloud communication channels, and local network exposure[7]. The **ZOSI 16CH Security Camera System** ($397.58) offers a complete DVR-based system with 2TB local storage and wireless battery cameras, suitable for studying hybrid local/cloud storage architectures and two-way audio channel security[27].
For smart lock analysis, the **NU-Set Eyecon** ($107.35, rated 2.4/5) is particularly noteworthy due to its low user rating and blockchain-secured photo key feature—a novel security claim that warrants cryptographic review[5]. Researchers should verify whether the "blockchain secure" implementation actually provides distributed consensus or is merely marketing language for a centralized key server.

## Network Infrastructure Components

The **Evertech 16 Channel Power Supply Box** ($45.95) and various range extenders[19] represent often-overlooked attack surfaces in the physical layer. Power distribution boxes may lack surge protection or isolation, creating potential for physical tampering or fire hazards. Wireless repeaters[19] can introduce man-in-the-middle opportunities if they improperly forward or decrypt traffic. Researchers should test whether these infrastructure components properly isolate network segments.

## Fingerprint and Biometric Security

The **SH-CHEN Fpm10A Fingerprint Module** ($17.38) represents the low-cost biometric sensor category increasingly found in smart locks[12]. Researchers should evaluate the optical sensor's resistance to spoofing (latent prints, gelatin molds) and examine the serial communication interface for plaintext transmission of biometric templates[12]. The FIPS 140-3 Level 3 encryption on devices like the **Apricorn Aegis Secure Key 3** ($168.87) provides a benchmark for comparing hardware-grade security against consumer smart home implementations[13].

## Methodology Recommendations

1. **Protocol Fuzzing**: Deploy fuzzing tools against Matter's IP-based communication and Zigbee's application layer to identify buffer overflows and state confusion bugs.
2. **Certificate Chain Analysis**: For Matter devices, validate the entire PKI chain from device attestation to cloud services, testing for expired, self-signed, or improperly validated certificates[1].
3. **Side-Channel Analysis**: Measure electromagnetic emissions and power consumption patterns during cryptographic operations on devices like the **Philips Hue** smart bulbs[24], which use Zigbee for communication.
4. **Update Mechanism Auditing**: Examine how devices receive firmware updates—particularly the **MyGica APC1967** Android TV box[28], which runs a full Android OS and may have unpatched vulnerabilities.
5. **Cloud API Interception**: For cloud-dependent devices like the **Deep Sentinel** system[6], intercept and analyze API calls between local hubs and monitoring centers.

## Ethical and Legal Considerations

Researchers must operate within legal boundaries, particularly when testing devices that connect to third-party monitoring services (e.g., Deep Sentinel's live guard service[6]). Obtaining proper authorization before penetration testing cloud infrastructure is essential. The algorithmic bias concerns noted in smart home AI systems[3] should also be considered when evaluating decision-making components like motion detection or facial recognition features.
