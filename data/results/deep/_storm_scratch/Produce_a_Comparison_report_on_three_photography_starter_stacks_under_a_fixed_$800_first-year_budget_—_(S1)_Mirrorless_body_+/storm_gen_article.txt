# Comparison of photography starter stacks under $800

## Overview of the three stacks

For a first-year photography budget capped at $800, three distinct starter stacks were evaluated: (S1) a mirrorless camera body with one prime lens and one zoom lens; (S2) a used DSLR body with two prime lenses and a flash; and (S3) a smartphone combined with a lens-attachment kit and a tripod, without a dedicated camera body. Each stack was assessed across five use cases: family photography, travel, low-light indoor events, portrait/close-up work, and video/vlogging[1][2][3].

## Stack S1: Mirrorless body + 1 prime + 1 zoom

The mirrorless option centers on a body such as the Canon EOS M50 (International Model) at roughly $37, paired with a prime lens like the VILTROX 33mm F1.4 for Fujifilm X-mount at $279 and a zoom like the Sony E PZ 16-50mm OSS at $178[3][4][5]. Total estimated cost falls near $494, well under the $800 cap. This stack offers strong autofocus performance and a compact form factor, making it suitable for family gatherings and travel. However, the prime lens provides a fixed focal length, which may limit flexibility in fast-paced shooting scenarios[1][6][7].

## Stack S2: Used DSLR body + 2 primes + flash

The DSLR stack assumes a used body such as the Nikon D3500 with 18-55mm lens (renewed or bundled) at roughly $9 to $16, two prime lenses (e.g., a 35mm f/1.8 and a 50mm f/1.8) totaling about $400, and a basic flash unit for around $50[2][8][9]. Total estimated cost is approximately $465 to $475. This setup excels in low-light conditions due to the wide apertures of the primes, and the flash adds versatility for indoor events. The DSLR's optical viewfinder and robust build are advantages for portrait work, but the system is bulkier than mirrorless alternatives[10][9][11].

## Stack S3: Smartphone + lens-attachment kit + tripod

The smartphone stack relies on a modern smartphone (e.g., an iPhone or Pixel) with a lens-attachment kit such as the Moment Blue Flare Anamorphic Lens at $40, plus a tripod like the UURig Handheld Grip at $10 or a motorized pan-tilt head at $74[12][13][14]. Total estimated cost is between $50 and $114, leaving substantial budget room for accessories or software. This stack is the most portable and cost-effective, leveraging computational photography for family snapshots and video. However, it lacks the sensor size and lens interchangeability of dedicated cameras, limiting performance in low light and for professional-level portraits[15][16][17].

## Use-case performance summary

- **Family photography (UC1)**: S1 and S2 both provide dedicated camera controls and image quality superior to S3, with S2's primes offering better subject isolation. S3 is convenient for quick sharing but struggles with indoor lighting[18][19][20].
- **Travel**: S1's compact mirrorless design is ideal, while S3's smartphone is always accessible. S2's bulk is a drawback[1][6][15].
- **Low-light indoor events**: S2's fast primes and flash outperform S1 and S3 significantly. S3 relies on digital processing[21][10][9].
- **Portrait/close-up work**: S2's prime lenses and flash provide the best control over depth of field and lighting. S1 is capable but limited by zoom aperture. S3's attachments offer only modest improvement[12][4][22].
- **Video/vlogging**: S3's smartphone excels with built-in stabilization and 4K autofocus webcam capabilities, but S1 and S2 can achieve better quality with external microphones and tripods[13][23][17].

## Budget considerations

All three stacks fit within the $800 first-year budget, with S3 leaving the most room for additional purchases (e.g., memory cards, bags, or editing software). S1 and S2 require careful selection of used or bundled items to stay under budget, as individual components can exceed $800 if purchased new[1][2][3]. Renewed or international models offer savings but may lack warranty support[24][25][5].
