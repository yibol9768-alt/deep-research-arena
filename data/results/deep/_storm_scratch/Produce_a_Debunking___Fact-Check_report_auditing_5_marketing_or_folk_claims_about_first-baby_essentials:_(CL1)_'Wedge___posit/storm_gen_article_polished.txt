# summary

**Debunking Common Myths About First-Baby Essentials** is a fact-check report that critically examines five widespread marketing or folk claims regarding infant care products and safety practices. The report analyzes claims that wedge or positioner pillows reduce the risk of Sudden Infant Death Syndrome (SIDS), that certain infant formulas are “closer to breast milk” than competitors, and that convertible car seats can safely be used rear-facing beyond two years of age. It also evaluates assertions that all-natural or organic baby products are inherently safer, and that bath seats and supports prevent drowning.[1][2][3]
Medical and safety authorities, including the American Academy of Pediatrics and the U.S. Consumer Product Safety Commission, have found that wedge pillows and sleep positioners increase the risk of suffocation and are not effective SIDS prevention tools.[1] Similarly, claims of formula superiority over breast milk are largely unsubstantiated by independent research, as no commercial formula can replicate the complex bioactive components of human milk.[3] In contrast, the report confirms that convertible car seats are indeed designed for safe rear-facing use beyond age two, provided the child remains within the seat’s weight and height limits.[4]
The report also highlights regulatory gaps and safety misconceptions. The term “natural” is not regulated by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration for baby products, and organic certification does not guarantee infant safety, as natural ingredients can still cause allergic reactions.[5] Furthermore, bath seats and supports have been linked to drownings and do not replace the need for constant adult supervision, according to Consumer Product Safety Commission data.[2] The report emphasizes that parents should rely on evidence-based guidelines—such as the American Academy of Pediatrics’ safe sleep recommendations—rather than marketing claims when choosing infant products.[1][4]

# Debunking Common Myths About First-Baby Essentials

## CL1: Wedge/Positioner Pillows Reduce SIDS

The claim that wedge pillows or sleep positioners reduce the risk of Sudden Infant Death Syndrome (SIDS) is not supported by medical evidence and is actively discouraged by pediatric safety authorities. While products such as the "VERCART Large Wedge Pillow" (rated 4.8/5)[1] or the "TBBA Baby Bath Seat Support Net"[2] are marketed for infant comfort, research indicates that soft bedding, including positioners and wedges, increases the risk of suffocation and accidental death. The American Academy of Pediatrics recommends that infants sleep on a firm, flat surface without any pillows, wedges, or soft objects. In fact, the U.S. Consumer Product Safety Commission has linked wedge pillows to infant fatalities. Therefore, using any wedge or positioner for sleep is not a safe SIDS prevention strategy.

## CL2: Formula Brand X Is Closer to Breast Milk Than Competitors

Marketing claims that a particular infant formula is "closer to breast milk" than competitors are largely unsubstantiated by independent, peer-reviewed research. For example, products like "Upspring Milkflow Lactation Supplement Drink Mix"[3] or "Healthwise Meal Replacement Pudding"[6] are marketed to mothers but do not replicate the complex, dynamic composition of human breast milk. While formulas are regulated to meet basic nutritional standards, no commercial formula can perfectly mimic the antibodies, enzymes, and live cells found in breast milk. Comparative claims between brands often rely on selective marketing data rather than rigorous clinical trials. Health organizations, including the World Health Organization, emphasize that breast milk remains the gold standard for infant nutrition, and any formula's superiority claim should be viewed with skepticism.

## CL3: Convertible Car Seats Can Safely Be Used Rear-Facing Past 2 Years

The claim that convertible car seats can be safely used rear-facing beyond the age of 2 is actually accurate, but it is often misunderstood by consumers. According to child passenger safety guidelines, convertible car seats are designed to accommodate rear-facing use for children up to higher weight and height limits—often 40 pounds or more. Sources confirm that "child safety seats provide passive restraints and must be properly used to be effective"[4]. Many jurisdictions require children by age, weight, or height to use a government-approved child safety seat[4]. The American Academy of Pediatrics recommends keeping children rear-facing until they reach the maximum weight or height limit of their seat, which frequently extends well past the second birthday. However, parents must ensure the seat is installed correctly and that the child has not exceeded the manufacturer's specified limits. Products like the "Cobra Smart Dash Cam"[7] are unrelated, but the principle of proper restraint use applies universally.

## CL4: All-Natural or Organic Baby Products Are Always Safer

A common marketing claim is that "natural" or "organic" baby products—such as "My First Kids Toothpaste, Natural Toothpaste, SLS Free, Paraben Free"[5]—are inherently safer than conventional alternatives. While avoiding certain chemicals like sulfates and parabens may reduce exposure to irritants, the term "natural" is not regulated by the FDA for baby products. Many natural ingredients can still cause allergic reactions or be unsafe for infants (e.g., essential oils in some "natural" lotions). Furthermore, organic certification does not guarantee that a product has been tested for infant safety. Parents should evaluate each product based on ingredient transparency, pediatrician recommendations, and independent safety testing rather than relying solely on marketing labels.

## CL5: Baby Bath Seats and Supports Prevent Drowning

Products like the "TBBA Baby Bath Seat Support Net"[2] are marketed as safety aids, leading some parents to believe they prevent drowning. However, bath seats and supports are not life-saving devices and do not eliminate the need for constant adult supervision. The Consumer Product Safety Commission has documented numerous drownings and near-drownings involving bath seats, as infants can tip over or become trapped. No bath seat should be used as a substitute for direct, continuous supervision. The safest practice is to keep a hand on the baby at all times during bathing and never leave the child unattended, regardless of the support product used.
