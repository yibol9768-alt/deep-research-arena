# Claim 1: '10 mg melatonin is more effective than 0.3 mg for sleep onset'
## Background
### Common dosage ranges in over-the-counter supplements
### Historical rationale for high-dose marketing
## Evidence Review
### Pharmacokinetics of melatonin: physiological (0.3–0.5 mg) vs. supraphysiological (10 mg) dosing
### Endogenous melatonin production rate and chronobiological rationale for low-dose efficacy
### Key meta-analyses comparing 0.3 mg, 3 mg, and 10 mg on objective polysomnography outcomes
### Dose-response curve: ceiling effect above 0.5 mg for sleep onset latency
### Side-effect profile of high doses: next-day grogginess, hormonal disruption, receptor desensitization
### Risk-benefit ratio: higher doses increase adverse events without improving latency
## Verdict
### Clinical guidelines (American Academy of Sleep Medicine, European guidelines)
### Practical recommendations: start with 0.3–0.5 mg, avoid doses above 3 mg for sleep onset
# Claim 2: 'Wrist-worn sleep trackers (Fitbit / Garmin / Apple Watch) measure sleep stages accurately'
## Background
### Technology used: actigraphy (accelerometry), heart rate variability, photoplethysmography
### Fundamental methodological gap: movement inference vs. neurophysiological measurement (EEG, EOG, EMG)
### Consumer marketing claims vs. clinical polysomnography (PSG) gold standard
## Evidence Review
### Validation studies comparing trackers to PSG: sample sizes, population demographics, statistical flaws
### Metrics analyzed: total sleep time, sleep onset latency, wake after sleep onset
### Systematic misclassification: quiet wakefulness mislabeled as light sleep (wake-to-light sleep confusion)
### Accuracy for REM vs. NREM deep sleep: algorithm limitations in detecting REM without eye movement sensors
### Performance in special populations: healthy young adults vs. insomnia, sleep apnea, shift workers
### Common statistical biases: small samples, convenience cohorts, epoch-by-epoch agreement limitations
## Verdict
### Accuracy for sleep duration (moderate) vs. sleep stages (poor)
### Population-specific limitations
### Practical recommendations: use for sleep duration trends only, not clinical sleep stage assessment
# Claim 3: 'Blue-light blocking glasses meaningfully improve sleep quality'
## Background
### Chronobiological mechanism: melanopsin-containing ipRGCs, melatonin suppression by short-wavelength light (~460 nm)
### Popularity of blue-blocking lenses: commercial claims vs. scientific evidence
## Evidence Review
### Controlled trials: blue-blocking glasses vs. placebo (clear lenses) with screen time controlled
### Objective sleep measures: polysomnography, actigraphy-derived sleep latency, total sleep time
### Subjective outcomes: sleep quality questionnaires, sleep onset perception
### Comparison with behavioral interventions: screen time reduction, device night mode settings
### Confounding factors: reduced screen time and pre-sleep relaxation behaviors among users
### Meta-analyses: effect size and clinical significance when controlling for behavioral confounds
### Conditions with strongest evidence: evening screen use in adolescents, shift work, delayed sleep phase disorder
## Verdict
### Effect size: small to moderate, often confounded by concurrent behavior change
### Clinical significance: meaningful for circadian rhythm disorders, marginal for general population
### Limitations and caveats: low-cost glasses may not block sufficient wavelengths; behavioral alternatives equally effective
# Claim 4: 'Melatonin is safe for long-term daily use in adults'
## Background
### Prevalence of chronic melatonin use (self-medication, unregulated supplement market)
### Regulatory status: dietary supplement (FDA) vs. drug in other jurisdictions
## Evidence Review
### Short-term safety profile: drowsiness, headache, dizziness, gastrointestinal symptoms
### Long-term studies (≥6 months): endocrine effects (prolactin, cortisol, sex hormones), immune modulation
### Impact on endogenous melatonin production: receptor regulation and potential tolerance
### Special populations: children (delayed puberty concerns), elderly (altered pharmacokinetics), pregnant women
### Drug interactions: anticoagulants, immunosuppressants, diabetes medications
## Verdict
### Risk-benefit assessment: generally safe for short-term use (<3 months); long-term data insufficient
### Gaps in longitudinal data: lack of large-scale, controlled trials beyond 12 months
### Recommendations from sleep medicine societies: use under medical supervision for chronic use
# Claim 5: 'Valerian root is an effective natural sleep aid with no side effects'
## Background
### Traditional use and popularity as an herbal remedy
### Common formulations: tea, capsules, tinctures; variability in active compound concentration
## Evidence Review
### Meta-analyses of valerian for insomnia: subjective sleep quality, sleep onset latency, total sleep time
### Active compounds: valerenic acid, valepotriates; proposed GABAergic mechanism
### Quality of evidence: small sample sizes, heterogeneity in dosing and formulations, publication bias
### Side effect profile: headache, dizziness, gastrointestinal issues, hepatotoxicity concerns (rare but documented)
### Comparison with placebo and prescription sleep aids: effect size smaller than benzodiazepines or Z-drugs
## Verdict
### Overall efficacy: minimal to modest improvement in subjective sleep quality; no consistent objective benefit
### Quality of evidence: low to moderate; insufficient for clinical recommendation
### Safety warnings: generally well-tolerated, but hepatotoxicity risk with prolonged use; drug interactions (CNS depressants, sedatives)

## Common Misconceptions
### Why these claims persist: marketing, anecdotal reports, confirmation bias, absence of regulatory oversight

### Red flags for pseudoscientific claims: lack of peer-reviewed evidence, exaggerated language, "natural" fallacy
