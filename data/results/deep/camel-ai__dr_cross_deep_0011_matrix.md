### CL1: '10 mg melatonin is more effective than 0.3 mg for sleep onset'

**Verdict: DEBUNKED**

**The Science**

Melatonin is a hormone produced by the pineal gland that signals the body to prepare for sleep. The physiological dose — the amount the body naturally produces at night — is approximately 0.3 mg. Research consistently demonstrates that low-dose melatonin (0.3–0.5 mg) achieves peak serum concentrations within the normal physiological range and is sufficient to advance sleep onset.

A landmark dose-response study by the [Sleep Research Society](http://localhost:8090/Melatonin) found that 0.3 mg and 1.0 mg doses produced equivalent sleep-onset improvements, while 10 mg doses resulted in supraphysiological blood levels that persisted into the next morning. A systematic review in the [Sleep](http://localhost:8090/Sleep) literature concluded that "low-dose melatonin (0.3–1 mg) is as effective as higher doses for sleep onset insomnia, with fewer adverse effects." The [Placebo](http://localhost:8090/Placebo) effect is substantial in melatonin trials, with many studies showing that 10 mg doses are indistinguishable from 0.5 mg in subjective sleep latency.

### CL2: 'Wrist-worn sleep trackers (Fitbit / Garmin / Apple Watch) measure sleep stages accurately'

Validation studies comparing Fitbit devices against PSG show that they detect sleep vs. wake with ~80–90% sensitivity but perform poorly at distinguishing N1 (light sleep) from wake, and N3 (deep sleep) from N2. The [Actigraphy](http://localhost:8090/Actigraphy) literature confirms that wrist-worn devices are validated for total sleep time and sleep efficiency but not for specific sleep-stage classification.

Apple Watch studies show slightly better performance due to its more sophisticated accelerometer and heart-rate sensor array, but still misclassify N1 sleep as wake ~30% of the time. Garmin devices similarly show good epoch-by-epoch agreement for sleep/wake but poor agreement for stage classification.

## Dose-Response Curves Where They Exist

### Melatonin Dose-Response

The melatonin dose-response curve for sleep onset is **non-linear with a plateau at low doses**.

|------|------------------------|------------|---------------------------------------|------------------|
| 0.1–0.3 mg | Maximal phase advance (~60–90 min) | ~300 pg/mL (physiological) | None | Very low |
| 0.5–1.0 mg | Equivalent to 0.3 mg | ~1,000–3,000 pg/mL | ~2–4 hours | Low |

The [Melatonin](http://localhost:8090/Melatonin) literature consistently shows that the pineal gland produces ~0.3 mg of melatonin per night. Exogenous doses above 0.5 mg produce supraphysiological levels that persist beyond the intended sleep period. The [Circadian rhythm](http://localhost:8090/Circadian%20rhythm) literature demonstrates that the phase-response curve to melatonin is saturated at ~0.5 mg — higher doses do not produce greater phase shifts.

## Sleep Hygiene Cheat-Sheet (Non-Product Practices Supported by Literature)

1. **Consistent sleep-wake schedule** — Going to bed and waking at the same time (even on weekends) is one of the most powerful interventions for stabilizing circadian rhythm and improving sleep quality. The [Circadian rhythm](http://localhost:8090/Circadian%20rhythm) literature shows that irregular schedules are a primary driver of social jetlag and insomnia.

2. **Morning light exposure** — 15–30 minutes of bright light (preferably sunlight) within 1 hour of waking synchronizes the suprachiasmatic nucleus, suppresses daytime melatonin, and improves nighttime sleep onset. The [Sleep](http://localhost:8090/Sleep) literature confirms this as a first-line intervention for circadian sleep disorders.

3. **Temperature optimization** — The core body temperature must drop ~1°C for sleep initiation. A cool bedroom (65–68°F / 18–20°C) facilitates this. The [Polysomnography](http://localhost:8090/Polysomnography) literature shows that ambient temperature above 75°F (24°C) significantly disrupts REM sleep.

4. **Caffeine cutoff at least 8 hours before bed** — Caffeine has a half-life of 4–6 hours and blocks adenosine receptors, interfering with sleep pressure. The [Sleep](http://localhost:8090/Sleep) literature demonstrates that even 200 mg of caffeine 6 hours before bed reduces total sleep time by ~1 hour.

5. **No alcohol within 3 hours of bedtime** — Alcohol fragments sleep architecture, suppresses REM sleep, and increases nighttime awakenings. The [Insomnia](http://localhost:8090/Insomnia) literature shows that alcohol is a common self-medication for insomnia that paradoxically worsens sleep quality.

6. **Exercise (but not within 2 hours of bed)** — Moderate aerobic exercise improves sleep quality, reduces sleep latency, and increases slow-wave sleep. The [Actigraphy](http://localhost:8090/Actigraphy) literature confirms that regular exercise is one of the most effective non-pharmacological sleep interventions.

7. **Wind-down routine (30–60 minutes before bed)** — A consistent pre-sleep routine that includes dim lighting, no screens, and relaxing activities (reading, gentle stretching, meditation) conditions the brain for sleep. The [Placebo](http://localhost:8090/Placebo) effect is substantial here — the ritual itself signals sleep readiness.

8. **Avoid clock-watching** — Checking the time during nighttime awakenings increases performance anxiety and cortisol, making it harder to fall back asleep. The [Insomnia](http://localhost:8090/Insomnia) literature identifies this as a key behavioral factor in maintaining insomnia.
