# Technology Landscape Report: Right to Repair — Open Source and Community Perspectives

## Introduction

The "Right to Repair" movement has emerged as one of the most consequential consumer-technology policy debates of the 2020s. At its core, the movement argues that device owners should have access to the parts, tools, documentation, and software necessary to fix their own products — whether those are tractors, smartphones, or graphics cards. This report examines the technology landscape through two complementary lenses: the **open-source software (OSS) ecosystem** on GitLab that empowers repair and hardware modification, and the **community discourse** on Reddit's /f/technology forum, where consumers, advocates, and skeptics debate the merits and challenges of repair legislation. Together, these sources paint a picture of a movement gaining momentum — but facing significant headwinds from entrenched corporate interests.

## Part 1: The Open-Source Ecosystem Supporting Repair

### ArduinoJson — Enabling Embedded Device Programming and Repair

On GitLab, the most relevant project at the intersection of open-source tooling and hardware repairability is **ArduinoJson** ([bblanchon/ArduinoJson](http://localhost:8023/bblanchon/ArduinoJson)), maintained by Benoît Blanchon. The project is described as a "JSON library for Arduino and embedded C++. Simple and efficient" and has accumulated **22 stars** with an active issue tracker containing **24 open issues** ([ArduinoJson project page](http://localhost:8023/bblanchon/ArduinoJson)).

While ArduinoJson is not a "repair tool" in the direct sense, it plays a critical infrastructural role in the broader repair ecosystem. Arduino and embedded platforms are the backbone of DIY electronics repair — from custom sensor replacements to aftermarket diagnostic tools. ArduinoJson's open issues reveal an active community grappling with real hardware constraints. For example, Issue #1883 discusses storing `DynamicJsonDocument` in PSRAM on ESP32 Wroover modules, a technical challenge directly relevant to building repair diagnostics for IoT devices ([Issue #1883](http://localhost:8023/bblanchon/ArduinoJson/-/issues/1883)). Issue #1804 requests "lazy value serialization" to handle memory-constrained scenarios like streaming JPEG data from ESP32 cameras — the kind of component that repair technicians regularly swap in consumer electronics ([Issue #1804](http://localhost:8023/bblanchon/ArduinoJson/-/issues/1804)). These discussions underscore that the open-source embedded ecosystem provides the foundational libraries that make third-party repair tools and diagnostics possible.

### nvidia-patch — Reclaiming Consumer Hardware Capabilities

A more directly repair-adjacent project is **nvidia-patch** ([keylase/nvidia-patch](http://localhost:8023/keylase/nvidia-patch)), which carries **26 stars** and **1 fork**. Its description is pointed: "This patch removes restriction on maximum number of simultaneous NVENC video encoding sessions imposed by Nvidia to consumer-grade GPUs" ([nvidia-patch project page](http://localhost:8023/keylase/nvidia-patch)). The project has **26 open issues**, reflecting an active community pushing back against Nvidia's artificial segmentation of consumer versus professional hardware.

This project embodies a core right-to-repair principle: **users should control the capabilities of hardware they own**. Nvidia's NVENC session limits are a software-imposed restriction on hardware that is physically capable of more — a form of planned obsolescence or market segmentation that repair advocates frequently cite. Issue #601, titled "Can the patch patterns be made more accessible for external use?", asks the maintainer to expose patch patterns in a machine-readable format (e.g., JSON) so that the patches can be integrated into package managers like Nix ([Issue #601](http://localhost:8023/keylase/nvidia-patch/-/issues/601)). This is a direct discussion about making repair-oriented software more **documented, accessible, and maintainable** — echoing the right-to-repair movement's emphasis on access to documentation.

Issue #587 requests a "GPUDirect RDMA patch" to enable a feature officially restricted to professional-tier cards, asking whether GeForce cards' exclusion is "a pure software limitation" — and if so, whether the community can remove it ([Issue #587](http://localhost:8023/keylase/nvidia-patch/-/issues/587)). These discussions map directly onto the right-to-repair ethos: hardware owners collaboratively investigating and restoring capabilities that manufacturers have arbitrarily restricted.

### Post 1: "11 states consider 'right to repair' for farming equipment"

Posted by user **Ranew** with a score of **5** and **112 comments**, this [AP News-sourced submission](http://localhost:9999/f/technology/113709/11-states-consider-right-to-repair-for-farming-equipment) generated robust debate about agricultural equipment repair. The top comment by **Rogueish1** (score 345) expressed a clear, populist stance: *"What's there to consider? People should be able to repair items they've purchased"* ([comment](http://localhost:9999/f/technology/113709/11-states-consider-right-to-repair-for-farming-equipment)).

However, deeper in the thread, the debate grew more nuanced. User **garlicroastedpotato** provided an extensive analysis of the John Deere monopoly dynamics, explaining how maintenance contracts are bundled into equipment purchases and how the US government protects John Deere as a strategic national brand. This user noted that "John Deere settled the issue" by allowing self-repair under new contracts — but those contracts require farmers to waive liability, use OEM parts, and not tamper with DEF emissions systems. The comment highlights a key tension: **settlements that appear to grant repair rights often come with significant strings attached** ([comment by garlicroastedpotato, score 12](http://localhost:9999/f/technology/113709/11-states-consider-right-to-repair-for-farming-equipment)).

User **fredlllll** raised the software lock issue: after replacing a failed switch, *"the machine tells you 'go to dealer to unlock'"* — software that "costs a lot of money or isn't even available to farmers." This directly parallels the nvidia-patch project's mission: software locks preventing hardware owners from using their own equipment.

### Post 2: "iFixit put up a Right to Repair billboard on the New York Governor's drive to work"

Posted by **kwiens** (iFixit CEO Kyle Wiens) with a score of **3** and **55 comments**, this [post](http://localhost:9999/f/technology/48682/ifixit-put-up-a-right-to-repair-billboard-on-the-new-york) discussed direct advocacy targeting New York Governor Kathy Hochul to sign the Digital Fair Repair Act.

User **glassFractals** provided the most detailed policy analysis (score 2), explaining that the bill would require companies selling products with electronic components in New York to "offer parts, tools, info, documentation, and software that allows owners and 3rd-party repair shops to service and repair products long-term." The commenter noted the bill's limitations — excluding "major home appliances, cars, farm equipment" — but called it "the most sweeping example of this sort of legislation so far in the US" and drew parallels to France's similar repairability law ([comment by glassFractals](http://localhost:9999/f/technology/48682/ifixit-put-up-a-right-to-repair-billboard-on-the-new-york)).

User **SirEDCaLot** (score 288) summed up the environmental argument: *"The less e-junk we have the better. Always better to fix than to replace."* Meanwhile, **Worried_Lawfulness43** identified a strategic opportunity: *"I feel like this is a matter climate activists and right to repair activists can work together on"* — suggesting that the movement's strongest path forward may be a coalition with environmental groups ([comment by Worried_Lawfulness43, score 5](http://localhost:9999/f/technology/48682/ifixit-put-up-a-right-to-repair-billboard-on-the-new-york)).

User **gazorpaglop** (score 13) described personal repair experiences — fixing a TV backlight, dishwasher leak, Nintendo Switch battery, and car components — and argued that *"repairability had to be engineered in from the beginning for everything."* This comment exemplifies the repair culture ethos that OSS projects like ArduinoJson and nvidia-patch support technically.

## Part 3: Analysis — How OSS Projects and Community Discussions Complement Each Other

The GitLab projects and Reddit discussions form two pillars of the same movement. **The OSS ecosystem provides the tools** — libraries like ArduinoJson that enable embedded diagnostics, and patches like nvidia-patch that restore artificially restricted hardware capabilities. **The Reddit community provides the demand signal and political will** — users sharing repair experiences, debating policy specifics, and pressuring legislators.

There are notable areas of **alignment**:

1. **Documentation access**: Reddit users repeatedly cite the need for manufacturer documentation, while nvidia-patch Issue #601 explicitly requests better documentation of patch patterns for external tooling. Both communities recognize that opaque, proprietary documentation is a primary barrier to repair.

2. **Software locks as anti-repair mechanisms**: The nvidia-patch project exists entirely because Nvidia uses software to restrict hardware capabilities. Reddit commenters describe identical patterns in farming equipment, where replacing a physical part triggers a software lock requiring dealer intervention. The OSS community's technical response (reverse-engineering and patching) directly addresses the problem the Reddit community identifies politically.

3. **Environmental framing**: Both communities converge on the e-waste argument. Reddit users frame repair as an environmental imperative, while the OSS ecosystem's commitment to extending hardware life (patching older GPUs, supporting legacy Arduino boards) operationalizes this ethos in code.

There are also **areas of tension**:

1. **Scope of legislation vs. scope of OSS**: Reddit commenters note that even well-intentioned legislation like New York's bill excludes major categories (appliances, cars, farm equipment). Yet OSS projects like nvidia-patch operate regardless of legislative scope — they address restrictions that no current bill would cover (GPU feature segmentation). This suggests the OSS community is more radical in practice than the legislative agenda the Reddit community is currently pushing.

2. **Individual vs. collective action**: Reddit user **SynbiosVyse** argued that consumers should *"vote with your wallet by purchasing products that respect your freedom"* — a market-based approach. But the nvidia-patch project implicitly contradicts this: when Nvidia dominates the GPU market, "voting with your wallet" is impractical. The OSS response is to modify the products regardless of manufacturer consent, reflecting a more confrontational philosophy.

3. **Corporate cooperation vs. adversarial hacking**: Some Reddit discussions suggest farmers should negotiate with John Deere, and that the company's settlement (allowing repair under new contract terms) is a step forward. The nvidia-patch project, by contrast, is entirely adversarial — it exists to circumvent Nvidia's restrictions without cooperation. This tension between collaboration and confrontation remains unresolved within the broader movement.

## Conclusion

The right-to-repair landscape is shaped by a symbiotic relationship between open-source technical infrastructure and grassroots political advocacy. On GitLab, projects like **nvidia-patch** (26 stars, 26 open issues) and **ArduinoJson** (22 stars, 24 open issues) provide the practical tools that make hardware modification and embedded repair feasible. On Reddit, the /f/technology community generates the discourse and political pressure that pushes legislators to codify repair rights into law — as seen in discussions around the 11-state farming equipment push and the iFixit billboard campaign in New York.

The OSS ecosystem is, in many ways, ahead of legislation: it already restores capabilities and provides documentation that right-to-repair bills would mandate. But legislation remains essential to normalize these rights for non-technical consumers who cannot apply GPU patches or write Arduino code. The most effective path forward — as both communities intuitively recognize — will require **simultaneous technical empowerment and regulatory enforcement**, ensuring that the right to repair is not merely a privilege of the technically skilled, but a universal consumer protection.
