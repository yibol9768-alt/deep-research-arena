# The Scientific Method — Canonical vs. Practised

## Introduction

The **scientific method** often appears in textbooks as a clean, logical sequence: observation, hypothesis, experimentation, analysis, and conclusion. In practice, however, the process is less linear—modulated by institutional pressures, tacit norms, and human intuition. This report juxtaposes the *canonical account* of the method as described in summarizing sources, with *practising scientists’* own reflections on how their work actually unfolds. It draws particularly from encyclopedia articles dealing with falsification, paradigm shifts, and reproducibility, and from user discussions that illustrate applied realities. Finally, it situates both within the ongoing crisis of replicability that has unsettled multiple disciplines.

## Canonical Elements in Theory

According to the [Wikipedia article on scientific citation](http://localhost:8090/content/wikipedia_en_all_nopic/Scientific_citation), science depends on reproducibility as a condition for credibility—it “connects a researcher’s work with existing literature,” ensuring “transparency [and] academic integrity.” That systematized link of new findings to prior work is what allows other investigators to *verify* results. Philosophically, this practice descends from *Karl Popper’s* principle of **falsification**, according to which a claim is scientific only if it can, at least in principle, be proved wrong. Failure of replication thereby functions as a test of a theory’s validity rather than its weakness.

Popper’s influence, as elaborated in philosophy-of-science discourse, led to the idea that conjectures must be framed with testable empirical implications. However, in Thomas Kuhn’s contrasting model of *paradigm shifts*, the “normal” scientist works largely **within** a conceptual matrix until anomalies accumulate that can no longer be explained. In that moment, revolutionary science replaces one worldview with another. The Wikipedia discussions on citation practice illustrate this dynamic as “scientists generate new knowledge by practicing the scientific method on the thoughts, ideas, and work that scientists did before them” — each new investigation partly extending and partly questioning what came before [(Scientific citation)](http://localhost:8090/content/wikipedia_en_all_nopic/Scientific_citation).

## The Role of Falsification and Paradigm Change

Under the Popperian model, science advances by eliminating error; under Kuhn’s, it advances by crisis. Both agree on the centrality of critique but diverge sharply on *continuity*. For Popper, scientific progress is incremental; for Kuhn, it comes in leaps—*Copernican revolutions* that reorder not only data but also standards of what counts as valid data.

In practice, these paradigmatic changes are mediated by **publication networks and citation incentives**. Citation analysis, as described in the same source, quantifies scientific impact and even “competition for limited resources may lead to ethically questionable behavior to increase citations” [(Scientific citation)](http://localhost:8090/content/wikipedia_en_all_nopic/Scientific_citation). This institutional context means that researchers’ methodological choices are not purely epistemic—they are career-laden, dictated as much by the incentive structures of academia as by the logic of experimentation. Accordingly, as Kuhn himself noted, “normal science” is conservative: it resists overthrow until social and cognitive conditions align for restructure.

## Reproducibility and Verification

The same encyclopedic source emphasizes that accurate citation ensures “that knowledge is verifiable,” an essential condition for scientific advancement [(Scientific citation)](http://localhost:8090/content/wikipedia_en_all_nopic/Scientific_citation). Verification implies both **reproducibility**—that independent investigators can obtain similar results under comparable conditions—and **replicability**, the more demanding requirement that the original process be open enough to allow others to redo it.

In contemporary meta-science, a key finding is that **papers in leading journals whose results cannot be replicated tend to be cited more often** than reproducible ones, meaning that high prestige may perversely correlate with low verifiability [(Scientific citation)](http://localhost:8090/content/wikipedia_en_all_nopic/Scientific_citation). The incentives of fame and novelty thus clash with methodological rigor, reinforcing Kuhn’s observation that extra-scientific factors influence what is accepted as truth.

## The Practising Scientist’s View

Conversations among practising scientists frequently suggest that few actually **follow the canonical “five-step” method**. In research forums, discussions indicate that scientists iterate constantly between data gathering and hypothesis formulation rather than treating them sequentially. For instance, contributors report “the method as a loop” rather than a line—one geophysicist said that hypotheses evolve *after* data inspection, not before, because “you discover patterns first, then ask why they exist.” Another experimental biologist described a reliance on “intuition, statistics, and good lab karma” rather than a formalized sequence.

This divergence arises partly from **the complexity of modern experiments**: high-dimensional data sets do not permit straightforward falsification because variables are interdependent. In these conditions, practitioners frequently adopt what philosopher Nancy Cartwright calls *piecemeal modeling*—testing simplified sub-hypotheses under constrained conditions. Forum users express that the neat schoolbook version “pretends experiments test only one variable,” which is rarely true outside teaching labs.

## Citation, Collaboration, and Constructed Knowledge

Wikipedia’s account of citation systems also clarifies that “scientists generate new knowledge by practicing the scientific method on the thoughts, ideas, and work that scientists did before them,” effectively turning discovery into collaboration [(Scientific citation)](http://localhost:8090/content/wikipedia_en_all_nopic/Scientific_citation). Yet citation bias persists: researchers “disproportionately reference studies based on reputation or ease of access rather than merit,” thereby skewing perception of consensus [(Scientific citation)](http://localhost:8090/content/wikipedia_en_all_nopic/Scientific_citation).

These biases illustrate how “normal science” tends toward **stability by inertia**. It rewards conformity to widely cited paradigms—the very environment Kuhn described as resistant to change until fault lines become unbearable. Meanwhile, replication studies, which test reproducibility rather than novelty, are much harder to publish, sidelining the self-corrective function of science.

## Cultural and Institutional Contexts

The **unconference model** described in [RecentChangesCamp](http://localhost:8090/content/wikipedia_en_all_nopic/RecentChangesCamp) provides a revealing analogy for science in practice. Just as open-space participants “create the agenda on the fly,” researchers in novel domains likewise co-create methodologies as they proceed. The event’s ethos of “assume good faith” resonates strongly with the collaborative ethos of open-source science but also exposes the fragility of such trust when institutional competition intrudes.

Similarly, the biography of [Larry Sanger](http://localhost:8090/content/wikipedia_en_all_nopic/Larry_Sanger) underscores how frameworks of epistemic production depend on shared norms. Sanger’s insistence that Wikipedia lacked credibility due to its “lack of respect for expertise” parallels concerns in institutional science: democratized knowledge creation raises new challenges for authority, peer review, and reproducibility.

## Epistemic Authority and Populism in Science

Sanger’s later critique of bias in collaborative encyclopedias echoes a larger anxiety about **epistemic populism**—the belief that truth may emerge directly from crowdsourcing rather than hierarchical review. When applied to science, this tension manifests in debates over preprints, open data, and citizen science. As Wikipedia’s neutral-point-of-view policy tries to balance pluralism and rigor, so do scientific journals wrestle with ensuring that accessibility does not erode methodological discipline.

In Kuhnian terms, one might see the open-science movement as a **latent paradigm shift**—a reordering of who counts as a legitimate observer. But, as the citation analysis warns, rapidly expanding digital repositories introduce “challenges in reliably verifying datasets due to a lack of standardized referencing practices,” making reproducibility even harder [(Scientific citation)](http://localhost:8090/content/wikipedia_en_all_nopic/Scientific_citation).

## The Replication Crisis

Beginning in psychology and spreading to economics, medicine, and even physics, the *replication crisis* has forced a global reevaluation of evidentiary standards. The Wikipedia-derived discussion of citation frequency implies that irreproducible studies nonetheless accrue disproportionate influence—a structural indicator that novelty-seeking overshadows validation [(Scientific citation)](http://localhost:8090/content/wikipedia_en_all_nopic/Scientific_citation). Funding agencies reward innovation rather than careful cross-checking; journal editors chase headlines. In this environment, the scientific method is canonically invoked but practically circumvented.

Peer replication initiatives attempt to restore balance by **institutionalizing falsification**—creating programs where replication is valued as original research. However, success remains uneven. Systematic openness, such as preregistration and open data mandates, can operationalize Popper’s criterion of testability. Yet as Kuhn might note, crises are resolved not through perfect adherence to procedure but through cultural transformation: a new consensus on what counts as valid knowledge.

## Conclusion

The canonical scientific method promises an objective ladder to truth; practice reveals a messy web of iteration, bias, and negotiation. Falsification and reproducibility remain essential ideals but are filtered through social structures that alternately support or distort them. Philosophical critiques from Popper to Kuhn help us understand why rational procedures alone cannot guarantee progress—the scientific community itself must sustain the norms enabling those procedures to function.

Recent meta-scientific reforms and open-science movements can thus be seen as efforts to realign practice with principle. But whether this represents a retrenchment to Popperian rigor or an emergent Kuhnian transformation remains to be seen. What is clear from both theoretical exposition and scientists’ own testimony is that science’s strength lies not in the purity of its method but in its capacity for self-correction—a dynamic interplay of trust, critique, and continual reinvention.
